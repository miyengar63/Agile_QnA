Scrum Theory

Scrum is built on Empiricism and Lean Thinking.

ConceptDescription
EmpiricismAsserts that knowledge comes from experience and making decisions based on what is observed
Lean ThinkingFocuses on maximizing value while minimizing waste

It is supported by 3 core pillars

PillarDescription
TransparencyMake the work visible and clear to everyone (those performing the work as well as those receiving the work)
InspectionRegularly review progress and outcomes (through scrum events)
AdaptationAdjust quickly to improve results (through scrum events)

Scrum delivers work in small, incremental steps, enabling continuous feedback, learning, and improvement

👉 In simple terms: Focus on impact, stay data-driven, and embrace continuous growth

What is Scrum?

Scrum is a lightweight framework that helps teams and organizations generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems.

AspectDescription
FrameworkNot a process or methodology — a simple framework within which you can employ various processes and techniques
PurposeHelps teams collaborate, learn, and adapt to deliver value incrementally in complex environments
StructureBuilt on a small team of people with three accountabilities: Developers, Product Owner, and Scrum Master
CadenceWork is organized into short, fixed-length cycles called Sprints (1–4 weeks) to ensure regular delivery
FoundationGrounded in empiricism and lean thinking — learn from experience, inspect reality, and adapt continuously

Scrum is intentionally incomplete — it defines only the parts required to implement Scrum theory, leaving the rest to the team.

👉 In simple terms: Scrum is a smart, structured way for teams to tackle complex work — delivering real value, fast, through teamwork and continuous learning

<--- Scrum Framework --->
QWhat is Scrum, and how does it differ from other agile frameworks?▼

Scrum is a lightweight Agile framework used to develop and deliver complex products through short, time-boxed iterations called Sprints (usually 1–4 weeks). It is based on empiricism, meaning decisions are made through transparency, inspection, and adaptation. Scrum clearly defines roles (Scrum Master, Product Owner, Developers), events (Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Review, Retrospective), and artifacts (Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, Increment) to help teams deliver value incrementally.

AspectScrumKanban
ApproachIterative, time-boxedContinuous flow
IterationsFixed-length SprintsNo Sprints
RolesDefined roles (Scrum Master, Product Owner, Developers)No mandatory roles
PlanningSprint Planning at start of SprintPlanning done continuously
Change HandlingChanges usually after Sprint endsChanges can be made anytime

Work LimitsLimited by Sprint commitmentWIP limits enforced
Best Use CaseEvolving requirements with regular deliveryContinuous work, support, maintenance
QWhat are the highlights of Scrum compared to other frameworks?▼
  • Iterative and Incremental Delivery: Scrum delivers working increments every sprint
  • Empiricism: Decisions are based on inspection, adaptation, and transparency
  • Defined Roles: Clear responsibilities with Scrum Master, Product Owner, and Developers.
  • Time-boxed Events: Structured ceremonies ensure focus and collaboration.
  • Focus on Value: Prioritized backlog ensures the team works on the highest-value items first.
  • Continuous Improvement: Retrospectives and metrics drive ongoing process and product enhancement.
  • Self-organizing Teams: Teams decide how to do the work, improving engagement and ownership.
QWhy is timeboxing important?▼
  • Creates focus: Limits time so teams concentrate on what truly matters.
  • Encourages predictability: Fixed durations help with planning and expectation setting.
  • Reduces risk: Problems surface early instead of late in the project.
  • Supports inspection & adaptation: Regular timeboxes enable frequent review and adjustment.
  • Prevents over-analysis: Drives decisions and progress instead of endless discussion.
  • Promotes sustainable pace: Avoids burnout by setting realistic boundaries.

QWhat is the role of a Scrum Master, and how does it differ from a Project Manager?▼
  • A Scrum Master facilitates the Scrum framework, removes impediments, and supports the development team in becoming self-organizing and high-performing.
  • A Project Manager plans, executes, and controls the project by managing scope, resources, timelines, and budgets.
QWhat’s the difference between project monitoring and controlling?▼

Monitoring is the process of observing and tracking project performance against the plan (passive/informational). Controlling is taking corrective actions when deviations occur to get the project back on track (active/corrective).

QIdeal goal of a Scrum Master?▼
  • Enable empiricism: Ensure transparency, inspection, and adaptation across the team and organization.
  • Build a self-managing, responsible team: Enable the team to own delivery and outcomes while the Scrum Master remains accountable for Scrum effectiveness.
  • Maximize value delivery: Enable consistent use of Definition of Ready and Definition of Done so the team delivers high-quality, valuable increments each sprint.
  • Promote Agile mindset & technical excellence: Encourage collaboration, continuous improvement, and sustainable engineering practices.
QTypical day as a Scrum Master?▼
  • Remove blockers fast : Help team unblock issues with tools, access, dependencies, or approvals
  • Improve clarity : Help refine unclear stories or acceptance criteria with PO/team
  • Keep focus in daily scrum : Guide team to talk about progress vs detailed problem-solving
  • Manage interruptions : Protect team from unplanned work or unnecessary stakeholder pressure
  • Improve collaboration : Encourage dev, QA, PO to talk directly instead of through SM
  • Reinforce simple Agile flow : Remind team to finish work before starting new items, avoid overloading

QExplain “Sprint Zero,” and when to use it?▼

Sprint Zero is a preparatory sprint before the first official development sprint. Its goal is to set up everything needed for smooth execution of future sprints.

Purpose / When to use:

  • Project setup: Configure tools, environments, CI/CD pipelines, and repositories.
  • Backlog preparation: Create initial Product Backlog items and high-level architecture.
  • Team onboarding: Introduce team members to the project, practices, and standards.
  • Spike or research work: Explore unknowns, prototypes, or feasibility studies.
QExplain Sprint Zero in your project context.▼
  • Sprint Zero concept : Initial phase before actual sprints to prepare team, environment, and backlog
  • Purpose : Enable smooth start of development, not to deliver business features
  • Setup activities : Environment setup, tools (Jira), CI/CD pipeline, access, basic architecture
  • Backlog readiness : Create initial epics, high-level stories, and prioritize with Product Owner
  • Definition setup : Establish Definition of Done, Definition of Ready, and working agreements
  • Caution : Should be time-boxed and not treated as a long planning phase or mini-waterfall
QManage daily stand-up meeting going beyond the time?▼
  • Enforce timeboxing: Keep the Daily Scrum strictly within 15 minutes by setting and reinforcing clear expectations.
  • Encourage focused updates: Guide the team to give brief updates on what was done, what’s next, and any blockers, keeping the discussion relevant.
  • Identify root causes: If the meeting frequently runs over, analyze whether it’s due to unnecessary detail or off-topic conversations.
  • Take discussions offline: Move detailed problem-solving or design discussions to separate follow-up conversations after the stand-up.
  • Coach continuously: Reinforce the purpose of the Daily Scrum—to quickly align, identify blockers, and keep the sprint on track.

<--- Commitments & Definitions --->
QWhat is a Definition of Done, and how does it relate to Scrum?▼

Definition of Done (DoD) is a shared checklist that defines when a product increment is considered complete and releasable.

  • It ensures quality and transparency across the team
  • Helps the team deliver a potentially shippable increment each sprint
  • Creates a common understanding between Developers, PO, and Scrum Master
  • Prevents partially done or hidden work from being counted as “done”

DoD Checklist:

  • Code completed and peer-reviewed
  • All relevant tests are passed
  • Acceptance criteria met
  • No critical defects open
  • Code merged and build successful
  • Deployed to required environment
  • Documentation updated (if needed)
  • Technical debt addressed or noted
QExplain the purpose and benefits of a “Definition of Ready” for user stories.▼

Definition of Ready (DoR) is a shared checklist that defines when a Product Backlog Item or user story is considered clear, well-defined, and ready to be pulled into a sprint.

  • It ensures clarity and shared understanding across the team
  • Helps the team pull only well-defined, actionable stories into a sprint
  • Creates a common agreement between Developers, PO, and Scrum Master on readiness
  • Prevents unclear, ambiguous, or unready work from entering the sprint

DoR Checklist:

  • User story clearly described and understood
  • Acceptance criteria defined and testable
  • Dependencies identified and addressed
  • Estimates provided (story points or time)
  • No blocking issues or external approvals pending
  • Design or mockups attached (if needed)
  • Performance, security, or other non-functional requirements identified
  • Story is small enough to be completed in a sprint
  • Team confirms story is ready to be pulled into a sprint
QWhat does the Definition of Done (DoD) mean in your team?▼
  • Shared quality standard : Agreed checklist that defines when work is truly “Done”
  • Completeness check : Ensures all coding, testing, and acceptance criteria are met
  • Quality assurance : Confirms unit testing, reviews, and necessary validations are completed
  • Usability readiness : Ensures item is potentially releasable at the end of sprint
  • Transparency : Same definition applies to all team members and all work items
  • Consistency : Prevents partial or “almost done” work from being accepted

QWho is responsible for creating the Definition of Done (DOD)?▼

The entire Scrum Team (Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Developers) is responsible for defining the DoD. If the organization provides a standard DoD, the team must adopt it as a minimum.

QDefine and use Acceptance Criteria?▼
  • Define clarity upfront : Product Owner and team agree on what “done correctly” means for a story
  • Break down expectations : Convert requirement into clear, testable conditions
  • Guide development : Developers use it to build the right functionality
  • Enable testing : QA validates each condition against expected behavior
  • Support conversation : Used during refinement to remove ambiguity early
  • Confirm completion : Story is accepted only when all criteria are met

QHow do you help the development team estimate and plan their work effectively?▼
  • Facilitate Backlog Refinement to break down stories into clear tasks
  • Guide Sprint Planning with Planning Poker
  • Assess velocity and capacity for realistic commitments
  • Encourage team collaboration and consensus on estimates
QCan you explain the difference between velocity and capacity in Scrum?▼
  • Velocity: past average work delivered (story points)
  • Capacity: team availability for upcoming Sprint
  • Key difference: Velocity forecasts delivery; capacity ensures realistic planning
QDefine capacity and velocity in project management, providing examples.▼
  • Capacity: Total available work a team can take in a Sprint based on time, availability, and focus. Example: 5 members available full Sprint → 5 × 10 days = ~50 person-days available effort
  • Velocity: Average amount of work (story points) a team completes in a Sprint. Example: Team consistently completes 28–32 story points per Sprint → velocity = ~30 points
  • Key difference: Capacity is input (availability) | Velocity is output (historical delivery)
  • Usage in planning: Capacity helps decide how much time is available | Velocity helps decide how much work fits
QDetails about size of current team and approach to calculating capacity?▼

Typically calculated by taking the sum of available hours for all developers in the Sprint and subtracting time for holidays, planned time-off, and non-development tasks like Scrum events.

QHow to conduct capacity planning?▼

Sum available developer hours minus ceremonies, leaves, and a buffer for administrative tasks.

QStory point estimation approach?▼
  • Shared understanding : Ensure everyone understands the story before estimating
  • Relative estimation : Compare work with known reference stories, not absolute time
  • Team-based estimation : Developers, QA, and PO discuss together for balanced input
  • Use planning poker : Encourage open discussion and alignment on differences
  • Focus on complexity : Consider effort, uncertainty, and risk—not just coding time
  • Align and finalize : Reach consensus and use it for sprint capacity planning
QEnsure estimations are accurate and meaningful?▼
  • Clear requirement : Ensure story is well understood before estimating
  • Proper refinement : Break down large or unclear stories into smaller, estimable items
  • Reference stories : Use past completed work as baseline for comparison
  • Team discussion : Encourage open conversation on complexity and unknowns
  • Include all roles : Dev, QA, and PO perspectives considered in estimation
  • Revisit if needed : Re-estimate when scope or understanding changes during refinement

QCan you explain the difference between a burndown chart and a burnup chart in Scrum?▼
AspectDetail
BurndownOnly shows work remaining. No separate line for scope vs progress. If scope changes, the line may jump, and the team often doesn't meet the ideal line by sprint end, making it harder to judge true progress
BurnupHas two lines — total scope and work completed. Even if scope changes, you can clearly see if the completed line is tracking toward the ideal progress, so it's easier to tell whether the team is on track

  • Involve customers: Engage them in backlog refinement, sprint reviews, and planning discussions.
  • Manage expectations: Set realistic commitments and clarify priorities.
  • Responsiveness: Address questions, concerns, and feedback promptly.
  • Focus on collaboration: Treat the customer as a partner, not just an approver.
QStrategies used to help a team continuously improve?▼
  • Regular Retrospectives: Use sprint retrospectives to identify what’s working and what needs improvement.
  • Inspect & Adapt: Use observations and metrics to refine processes and improve delivery.
  • Embrace a Feedback Culture: Treat feedback from both success and failure as opportunities to learn and grow.
  • Turn Feedback into Action: Convert improvement ideas into clear, shared action items and track their effectiveness.
  • Metrics Review: Review velocity, cycle time, defect trends, and sprint goal success to spot bottlenecks.
  • Refine DoD and DoR: Continuously improve Definition of Done and Definition of Ready to ensure clarity and quality.
  • Promote Technical Excellence: Encourage code reviews, automation, refactoring, and reduction of technical debt.
  • Encourage Knowledge Sharing: Support cross-skilling, pairing, and learning sessions.

QHow do you handle resistance to Agile and Scrum?▼
  • Listen first: Talk to stakeholders to understand fears like loss of control or deadlines.
  • Explain the why: Show how Scrum improves faster delivery and transparency using real examples.
