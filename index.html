Scrum Theory

Scrum is built on Empiricism and Lean Thinking.

ConceptDescription
EmpiricismAsserts that knowledge comes from experience and making decisions based on what is observed
Lean ThinkingFocuses on maximizing value while minimizing waste

It is supported by 3 core pillars

PillarDescription
TransparencyMake the work visible and clear to everyone (those performing the work as well as those receiving the work)
InspectionRegularly review progress and outcomes (through scrum events)
AdaptationAdjust quickly to improve results (through scrum events)

Scrum delivers work in small, incremental steps, enabling continuous feedback, learning, and improvement

👉 In simple terms: Focus on impact, stay data-driven, and embrace continuous growth
×

What is Scrum?

Scrum is a lightweight framework that helps teams and organizations generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems.

AspectDescription
FrameworkNot a process or methodology — a simple framework within which you can employ various processes and techniques
PurposeHelps teams collaborate, learn, and adapt to deliver value incrementally in complex environments
StructureBuilt on a small team of people with three accountabilities: Developers, Product Owner, and Scrum Master
CadenceWork is organized into short, fixed-length cycles called Sprints (1–4 weeks) to ensure regular delivery
FoundationGrounded in empiricism and lean thinking — learn from experience, inspect reality, and adapt continuously

Scrum is intentionally incomplete — it defines only the parts required to implement Scrum theory, leaving the rest to the team.

👉 In simple terms: Scrum is a smart, structured way for teams to tackle complex work — delivering real value, fast, through teamwork and continuous learning

Scrum Team

AccountabilityPrimary Focus & Description
DevelopersCreating the Increment. They are the people in the Scrum Team committed to creating any aspect of a usable Increment each Sprint. This includes coding, testing, designing, or any work needed to meet the Definition of Done.
Product Owner (PO)Maximizing Value. They are accountable for effective Product Backlog management, including developing and communicating the Product Goal and ensuring the backlog is transparent and understood.
Scrum Master (SM)Scrum Effectiveness. They are accountable for establishing Scrum as defined in the Scrum Guide. They do this by helping everyone understand Scrum theory and practice, both within the Scrum Team and the organization.

Topic Methodology vs Framework ▼
AspectMethodologyFramework
Prescriptive vs. FlexiblePrescriptive, strict processFlexible, provides structure but allows adaptations
Execution StylePrescribes exact stepsDefines structure but allows variations
ExampleWaterfall, Six SigmaScrum, SAFe, LeSS
  • Agile focuses on "What" and "Why" — values and principles that guide development.
  • Scrum focuses on "How" — a structured yet adaptive way to apply those principles in real-world delivery.
  • Agile is neither a methodology nor a framework — it is a mindset built on values and principles defined in the Agile Manifesto. It emphasizes adaptability, collaboration, and delivering value continuously.
  • Scrum, on the other hand, is a framework that implements Agile principles in practice. It defines roles, events, and artifacts, but leaves flexibility for teams to decide how to execute the work.
👉 In simple terms: Agile is the mindset of working smart and adapting to change, while Scrum is a practical framework that helps teams implement Agile in a structured way.

Topic Scrum Master vs Project Manager – Key Differences ▼

Scrum Master

  • Facilitates Scrum framework and ensures it is followed correctly
  • Removes impediments that block the team’s progress
  • Enables and coaches the team to become self-organizing and high-performing
  • Focuses on team collaboration, Agile practices, and continuous improvement
  • Serves the Scrum Team and organization as a servant leader

Project Manager

  • Plans, executes, and controls the overall project delivery
  • Manages scope, schedule, resources, and budget
  • Directs and assigns work to ensure project completion
  • Focuses on delivery timelines, cost control, and project governance
  • Acts as a manager responsible for project success and reporting
Topic Project Monitoring vs Project Control ▼

Monitoring

  • Observing and tracking project performance against the plan (passive / informational)

Controlling

  • Taking corrective actions when deviations occur to bring the project back on track (active / corrective)

Definition of Done (DoD) Definition of Done (DoD) – Overview and Checklist ▼
AspectDetails
DefinitionDefinition of Done (DoD) is a shared checklist that defines when a product increment is complete, meets quality standards, and is potentially releasable
Shared Quality StandardAgreed understanding across Developers, Product Owner, and Scrum Master on what “Done” means
Completeness CheckEnsures coding, testing, and all acceptance criteria are fully completed
Quality AssuranceConfirms peer reviews, unit testing, and validations are completed with no critical defects
Usability / Release ReadinessEnsures the increment is potentially shippable and deployable at the end of the sprint
TransparencyCreates visibility and a common understanding across the team
ConsistencyPrevents partially done or “almost done” work from being accepted

DoD Checklist

  • Code completed and peer-reviewed
  • All tests passed
  • Acceptance criteria met
  • No critical defects open
  • Code merged and build successful
  • Deployed to required environment
  • Documentation updated (if needed)
  • Technical debt addressed or noted
Definition of Ready (DoR) Definition of Ready (DoR) – Overview and Checklist ▼
AspectDetails
DefinitionDefinition of Ready (DoR) is a shared checklist that defines when a Product Backlog Item or user story is considered clear, well-defined, and ready to be pulled into a sprint
Clarity & UnderstandingEnsures clarity and shared understanding across the team
Sprint ReadinessHelps the team pull only well-defined, actionable stories into a sprint
Shared AgreementCreates a common agreement between Developers, Product Owner, and Scrum Master on readiness
Prevention of AmbiguityPrevents unclear, ambiguous, or unready work from entering the sprint

DoR Checklist

  • User story clearly described and understood
  • Acceptance criteria defined and testable
  • Dependencies identified and addressed
  • Estimates provided (story points or time)
  • No blocking issues or external approvals pending
  • Design or mockups attached (if needed)
  • Performance, security, or other non-functional requirements identified
  • Story is small enough to be completed in a sprint
  • Team confirms story is ready to be pulled into a sprint

TopicCapacity and Velocity in Project Planning with Examples▼
AspectDescription
CapacityTotal available work a team can take in a Sprint based on time, availability, and focus. Example: 5 members available full Sprint → 5 × 10 days = ~50 person-days
VelocityAverage amount of work (story points) a team completes in a Sprint. Example: ~30 story points per Sprint
Key differenceCapacity is input (availability) | Velocity is output (historical delivery)
Usage in planningCapacity defines available effort | Velocity defines expected delivery
TopicSprint Capacity Calculation Approach▼

Capacity is calculated by adding total available hours of all team members for the Sprint and subtracting holidays, planned time-off, and non-development activities like Scrum events.

TopicCapacity Planning in Scrum▼

Sum available developer hours minus ceremonies, leaves, and a buffer for administrative tasks.

Topic Burndown vs Burnup Charts (Comparison and Sprint Burnup Concept) ▼
AspectDetail
Burndown chartTracks remaining work over time → shows how much is left to complete
Burnup chartTracks completed work against total scope → shows progress toward goal
Key differenceBurndown focuses on remaining work | Burnup shows both scope and completed work
Scope visibilityBurndown hides scope changes | Burnup clearly shows scope increase/decrease
Sprint burnupTracks completed work within a sprint against total committed scope
Lines in burnupOne line for total scope, one line for completed work
BenefitClearly shows progress and any scope change during the sprint

TopicHandling Team Solution Conflict (Option A vs Option B)▼
AspectDetail
Listen firstUnderstand why the team chose Option A (constraints, assumptions, risks, context)

Facilitate discussionBring team together to compare options openly, not as debate but as shared problem-solving
Challenge constructivelyShare concerns on Option A with facts/data, not authority or opinion
Encourage ownershipLet developers own the final technical decision since they build and maintain it
Align on outcome, not egoFocus on solving the problem effectively rather than "winning" an option

TopicHandling Team Overcommitment▼
ActionDescription
Use historical dataBring velocity, past commitments vs completions, and spillover data to ground planning in facts
Plan by capacity, not optimismFactor in team availability, holidays, support work, and interruptions before committing

AspectDetail
Requirement stabilityStable & predictable work → Kanban | Evolving & uncertain → Scrum
Delivery patternContinuous flow → Kanban | Time-boxed delivery → Scrum
Prioritization needDynamic prioritization → Kanban | Sprint-based planning → Scrum
Change frequencyFrequent ad-hoc changes → Kanban | Controlled changes → Scrum
Team maturityOperational/support teams → Kanban | Cross-functional product teams → Scrum
Commitment styleNo fixed commitment → Kanban | Sprint commitment required → Scrum
